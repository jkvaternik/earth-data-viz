CO2 Emissions

Background

CO2 emissions are the main contributor to global warming on Planet Earth, which has several other sideaffects such as melting ice caps, more severe weather, and bleaching of coral reefs. The main source of CO2 comes from the burning of fossil fules. Therefore, in this section we will focus on carbon emissions and energy production for every country contributing to our planetary crisis.

    • Process

    In this section, I display the process of coming up with my final visualizations.

    Energy Production Over Time

    For this visualization, I originally tried creating a bar chart for each country, and having a user input scroll bar to choose what year the bar charts represented. The idea was to convey how the distributions of energy types changed over time.

    The issue with this visualization is that it did not effectively show how the distributions changed over time. I ended up using area charts for each country to show the distributions of energy production over time.

    What contributes to CO2 emissions.

    In this visualization, I wanted to show which energy types contributed most to CO2 emissions. I attempted to do this by normalizing the data by calculating the standard deviations of CO2 and all of the energy types.

    This issue with this visualization was that there was no clear way to interpret the fluctuations in the lines since my unit was a standard deviation. I ended up displaying CO2 emissions and the distributions of energy types next to each other so one could compare them.

    Background

    Much of the world's landamass is covered by forest. Forests account for 38% of all habitable land. That is over one-third of habitable land and around one qarter of the total landmass on this planet! This is a signinfant decrease from what it used to be. Currently half of all habitable land is used for agriculture. OurWorldInData

    Net Forestation Changes

    Lets try to understand that decrease. We can think of forestation in terms of deforestation (loss of forest) and afforestation (gain of forest). Afforestation includes both natural forest expansion and supernatural expansion at the hands of humans. Thus, net forest change is not the same as defortestation, it inlcudes both losses and gains. Here we can see the net changes in forest cover from 1990 to 2015. Countries shown in yellow have experienced net deforestation, countires shown in blue have experienced net afforestation.

    Brazil has experienced the most deforestation. China has experienced the most afforestation.

    Despite afforestation in some regions, it is not enough to make up for the loss of forest elsewhere. If we want to preserve this natural rescource changes to our current agricultrual practices will need to be made.

    Background

    The effects of climate change and unsustainable use can be found in our oceans, lakes, and rivers. While water is essential to human life, it can also lead to dreadful consequences — land mass sinking coupled with rising sea levels can be the end of many coastal cities. Yet at the same time, freshwater resources have become scarce due to unsustainable water usage combined with the booming trend of human population. The following sections look at the effects created by rising sea levels as well as the results of human water consumption.

    Part I: Ports vs. Rising Sea Levels

    With a growing human population and increasing temperatures, sea levels have been rising at an increasing rate since the early 1900s. The following visualization presents sea level data collected from CSIRO (Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation).

    Dataset: Global Average Absolute Sea Level Change, 1880-2014

    Coupled with the sinking of land masses, rising sea-levels have the potential of flooding multiple cities and impacting the seafood economy. Here we can look at how rising sea levels could flood cities and surrounding major ports — leaving them unable to function. Researchers and scientists have warned about the consequences of global warming towards rising sea levels. According to a study by Scott A. Kulp and Benjamin H. Strauss, sea levels are projected to rise by 2 meters at the end of the twenty-first century under high carbon emissions. How would this affect major U.S. ports? Who would be affected? The following visualization displays the revenue of major US ports in millions of dollars. If we assumed a certain sea level change, would the port be unusuable due to rise in sea level? Which sea levels will affect the sea industry the most?

    Datasets: NOAA Fisheries Top US Ports Dataset, USGS Topagraphy

    At the 2 meters suggested by Kulp and Strauss, the U.S. loses roughly $472.2 million due to the instability of these regions caused by rising sea levels. Additionally, even with sharp, immediate cuts to carbon emissions, it could rise another 0.5 m this century — jeopardizing $192.1 million of the seafood economy. This could only worsen as the same circumstances would extend to other countries as well — impacting the global seafood chain.This issue will require innovative solutions to mitigate our effect on increasing temperatures and increasing sea levels to maintain communities and economies afloat.

    Water Use

    Water is a two-sided coin. While mass bodies of water have the potential of flooding ports and cities, we also need freshwater access to survive. Yet, due to growing human populations, water scarcity has become more of an issue. In recent years, South Africa was warned of Day Zero, a response to their water crisis in 2018. Unless water consumption patterns changed, scientists predicted that the countries freshwater supply would end. However, this water crisis extends farther than South Africa, as 5.0 of 9.7 billion people (or 52% of the global population) in the world will be living in water stressed regions by 2050. So, which regions are currently experiencing higher levels of water stress?

    Dataset: UN — Level of water stress: freshwater withdrawal as a proportion of available freshwater resources

    Many countries with a high freshwater withdrawal of available freshwater resources are located in arid regions, which leads to the question — what can be done? Using this information, let's turn to global water withdrawal rates to see which countries need to change their water consumption patterns to avoid running out of water.

    Datasets: UN — Total water withdrawal per capita, UN — Gross domestic product (GDP), per capita (USD)

    In the visualization above, countries are mapped by their annual total water withdrawal per person in cubic meters by their respective GDP per capita. The sustainable freshwater withdrawal benchmark was suggested by Arjen Hoekstra, a reknowned professor of water management at the University of Twente, Enschede, the Netherlands based on projected population grown and available water resources. Now, let's add another measure: a GDP benchmark for developing countries to see which if more developing countries or developed countries support an unsustainable water consumption:

    Interestingly, there are more developed countries with unsustainable water consumption patterns than developing countries. One possible reason is the consumer culture in these countries. Our freshwater withdrawal consists of two types of water; indirect and direct water consumption. While water conservation tips such as taking shorter showers and turning off running water while brushing your teeth or washing the dishes aim to reduce direct consumption, it's our indirect consumption that hinders our sustainable efforts. Unhealthy, meat-heavy diets and consumerist patterns are large perpetrators to our high water consumption. So, how do these countries fare with water scarcity? Are more countries in water stressed conditions? Let's turn back to our global visualization with a focus on these countries with unsustainable water consumption patterns.

    While more developed countries have unsustainable water consumption rates, those same countries do not appear to be in high or critical water-stress conditions (<50%). Unfortunately, these countries may not realize the consequences of their actions yet, but could be in water stress conditions based on their current trajectory. We can also observe the water consumption and water stress conditions of the countries with lower GDP versus higher GDP in the next visualization.

    Countries with lower GDP are typically in more stressed water conditions (>50%) with the exception of Indonesia. Water scarcity can have effects on a countries social and economic health as water becomes more of a priority. Residents of these regions must focus more on retrieving water rather than economic or social productivity. However, through the help of neighboring countries and the reduction of their water consumption to a sustainable level, these regions could benefit socially and economically.
