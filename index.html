Background

CO2 emissions are the main contributor to global warming on Planet Earth, which has several other sideaffects such as melting ice caps, more severe weather, and bleaching of coral reefs. The main source of CO2 comes from the burning of fossil fules. Therefore, in this section we will focus on carbon emissions and energy production for every country contributing to our planetary crisis.

    • Process

    In this section, I display the process of coming up with my final visualizations.

    Energy Production Over Time

    For this visualization, I originally tried creating a bar chart for each country, and having a user input scroll bar to choose what year the bar charts represented. The idea was to convey how the distributions of energy types changed over time.

    The issue with this visualization is that it did not effectively show how the distributions changed over time. I ended up using area charts for each country to show the distributions of energy production over time.

    What contributes to CO2 emissions.

    In this visualization, I wanted to show which energy types contributed most to CO2 emissions. I attempted to do this by normalizing the data by calculating the standard deviations of CO2 and all of the energy types.

    This issue with this visualization was that there was no clear way to interpret the fluctuations in the lines since my unit was a standard deviation. I ended up displaying CO2 emissions and the distributions of energy types next to each other so one could compare them.

    Forests and Deforestation

    Background

    Much of the world's landamass is covered by forest. Forests account for 38% of all habitable land. That is over one-third of habitable land and around one qarter of the total landmass on this planet! This is a signinfant decrease from what it used to be. Currently half of all habitable land is used for agriculture. OurWorldInData

    Net Forestation Changes

    Lets try to understand that decrease. We can think of forestation in terms of deforestation (loss of forest) and afforestation (gain of forest). Afforestation includes both natural forest expansion and supernatural expansion at the hands of humans. Thus, net forest change is not the same as defortestation, it inlcudes both losses and gains. Here we can see the net changes in forest cover from 1990 to 2015. Countries shown in yellow have experienced net deforestation, countires shown in blue have experienced net afforestation.

    Brazil has experienced the most deforestation. China has experienced the most afforestation.

    Despite afforestation in some regions, it is not enough to make up for the loss of forest elsewhere. If we want to preserve this natural rescource changes to our current agricultrual practices will need to be made.

    The State of Water

    Background

    The effects of climate change and unsustainable use can be found in our oceans, lakes, and rivers. While water is essential to human life, it can also lead to dreadful consequences — land mass sinking coupled with rising sea levels can be the end of many coastal cities. Yet at the same time, freshwater resources have become scarce due to unsustainable water usage combined with the booming path of human population. How bad is it? Let’s look at the data.

    Sea Levels

    It's been made publicly aware by scientists that sea levels are on the rise due to global warming. But how fast is the sea level rising?

    So how do these sea levels affect us? Specifically, let's look at the production of seafood in the United States.     Water Use

    Water is a two-sided coin. While mass bodies of water have the potential of flooding ports and cities, we also need freshwater access to survive. Yet, due to human overpopulation, water scarcity has become more of an issue. In recent years, South Africa was noticed of Day Zero, a response to their water crisis. Unless water consumption patterns changed, scientists predicted that the countries freshwater supply would end. Water scarcity is an issue all over the globe and not just in South Africa. So we must ask ourselves, who has the most sustainable water consumption?
