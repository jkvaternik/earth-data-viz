CO2 Emissions

Background

CO2 emissions are the main contributor to global warming on Planet Earth, which has several other sideaffects such as melting ice caps, more severe weather, and bleaching of coral reefs. The main source of CO2 comes from the burning of fossil fules. Therefore, in this section we will focus on carbon emissions and energy production for every country contributing to our planetary crisis.

Example Insights

Insight 1

Primary Insight: The China has become the highest contributor to global CO2 emissions since the year 2000, followed by the United States and India

Secondary Insight: Overall, CO2 emissions have been increasing over time.

Insight 2

Primary Insight: For the top three CO2 emitting countries, coal production contributes the most to CO2 emissions out of gas, petroleum, and nuclear and other.

Secondary Insight: There is a correlation between energy production and CO2 emissions over time.

Insight 3

Primary Insight: The United States and China have reduced their coal production since 2005, while India has slightly increased it.

Secondary Insight: China has increased its nuclear production to replace coal and petroleum, and India has increased its nuclear production to replace gas and petroleum.

    Net Forestation Changes

    Lets try to understand that decrease. We can think of forestatio in terms of deforestation (loss of forest) and afforestation (gain of forest). Afforestation includes both natureal forest expansion and supernatural expansion at the hands of humans. Thus, net forest change is not the same as defortestation, it inlcudes both losses and gains. Here we can see the net changes in forest cover from 1990 to 2015. Countries shown in yellow have experienced net deforestation, countires shown in blue have experienced net afforestation.

    The State of Water

    Background

    The effects of climate change and unsustainable use can be found in our oceans, lakes, and rivers. While water is essential to human life, it can also lead to dreadful consequences — land mass sinking coupled with rising sea levels can be the end of many coastal cities. Yet at the same time, freshwater resources have become scarce due to unsustainable water usage combined with the booming path of human population. How bad is it? Let’s look at the data.

    Water Use

    Water is a two-sided coin. While mass bodies of water have the potential of flooding ports and cities, we also need freshwater access to survive. Yet, due to human overpopulation, water scarcity has become more of an issue. In recent years, South Africa was noticed of Day Zero, a response to their water crisis. Unless water consumption patterns changed, scientists predicted that the countries freshwater supply would end. Water scarcity is an issue all over the globe and not just in South Africa. So we must ask ourselves, who has the most sustainable water consumption?
